# RoverDevKit: An AI-Augmented Tradespace Exploration Tool for Lunar Micro-Rover Conceptual Design

## Project Brief

---

## 1. Introduction

The conceptual design of a planetary rover is a coupled, high-dimensional, multi-objective problem. Wheel geometry affects mobility, which sets motor torque requirements, which sets electrical loads, which sizes the battery and solar array, which drives chassis mass, which feeds back into wheel sinkage and traction — and every link in this chain depends on a mission profile (latitude, traverse distance, terrain class, sun geometry) that the designer often does not get to choose. Resolving these trades is currently the work of small teams of expert engineers using either proprietary concurrent-engineering facilities such as NASA JPL's Team X (Case et al., 2021; Sherwood, 2014) and ESA's CDF, or ad-hoc spreadsheet models that do not generalize between studies.

Lunar rover conceptual design is not just a harder version of terrestrial vehicle design — it differs in ways that make ML-accelerated tradespace exploration more valuable here than in mature ground-vehicle domains. First, the mobility-power coupling is far tighter than in terrestrial vehicles. Chemical fuel has roughly 100× the specific energy of a lithium-ion battery and on the order of 10,000× the instantaneous output of a solar panel of equivalent mass; this energy abundance lets terrestrial designers size the powertrain almost independently of the chassis, because additional fuel can always be carried. On a solar-powered lunar rover, every additional watt drawn by the wheels must be generated by panel area that must itself be carried, supported by structure that must be carried, on regolith whose sinkage scales with the resulting mass. The design loop closes hard, which is precisely the regime in which coupled tradespace tools earn their value over decoupled spreadsheet trades. Second, mobility failures are unrecoverable: a stuck terrestrial prototype is winched out, while a rover that exceeds its sinkage limit ends its mission. This shifts the value of the conceptual design tool from finding a good point design within an assumed-feasible region to *characterizing the feasible region itself*, which is exactly what surrogate-driven exploration with explicit constraint maps delivers. Third, mission profile drives the optimal design more strongly than vehicle class does — a 10 kg polar prospector and a 10 kg equatorial mare rover converge on materially different wheel and power configurations because of sun geometry and thermal cycling, not because of anything intrinsic to the vehicle. Fourth, the operating regime sits outside the calibration envelope of mature terrestrial off-road mobility codes (e.g., NRMM): at 1/6 g, normal stresses on the soil are far lower, the slip-sinkage relationship shifts, and grouser effectiveness changes. Finally, the design space is genuinely under-explored — only on the order of eight lunar rovers have flown, against a century of accumulated automotive engineering intuition — so the human-rules-of-thumb baseline that an ML tool must beat is much weaker here than in mature vehicle domains.

This project proposes **RoverDevKit**, an open-source, AI-augmented tradespace exploration tool that targets the *Pre-Phase A / conceptual design* stage of the lunar micro-rover lifecycle. The core idea is to chain together physics-based sub-models for terramechanics, solar power, battery state-of-charge, mass estimation, and traverse simulation into a single mission evaluator, then train a machine-learning surrogate over the coupled wheel-chassis-power design space so that thousands of candidate designs can be screened in seconds rather than hours. The use of ML here is not novelty for its own sake — surrogate modeling has been a standard tool in aerospace conceptual design for two decades (Ross & Hastings, 2005; Dasari et al., 2019) — but the combination of (a) an open-source, fully-cited mission evaluator, (b) a multi-fidelity surrogate that grounds itself in higher-fidelity terramechanics simulation when available, and (c) a validation strategy built around rediscovering the design points of real flown lunar micro-rovers is, to my knowledge, not currently available as a published artifact. The significance of AI augmentation at this stage is that it changes the *nature* of conceptual design from "evaluate the few candidate architectures we had time to model" to "characterize the entire feasible region and let the optimizer find the corners we would not have thought to look at."

## 2. Problem Description

The specific problem is the **coupled co-design of mobility and power subsystems for a lunar micro-rover (5–50 kg class)** under a fixed mission profile, where the design vector spans wheel geometry (radius, width, grouser height and count, number of wheels), chassis parameters (mass, wheelbase), power architecture (solar array area, battery capacity, avionics power draw), and operations (nominal drive speed, drive duty cycle). The mission evaluator must return mission-level performance metrics — traverse range, energy margin, slope capability, total mass, peak motor torque, maximum sinkage — together with binary constraint checks on motor torque limits and thermal survival.

This sits at the **Pre-Phase A / Concept Maturity Level 2–4** stage of the systems engineering lifecycle, where the design space is still wide open and the cost of changing decisions is small (Sherwood, 2014). Augmentation at this stage is more leveraged than at any other point in the lifecycle for three reasons. First, decisions made at conceptual design lock in roughly 70–80% of the eventual mission cost, even though only a few percent of the budget has been spent — so improving decision quality here has outsized impact. Second, the conceptual design stage is also where the design space is highest-dimensional and least-constrained, which is exactly where naive human search performs worst and where surrogate-driven optimization can find non-obvious Pareto-optimal designs. Third, the existing tools for this stage are either proprietary (Team X, CDF) and therefore unavailable to most academic researchers and small mission teams, or they are static spreadsheet models that cannot explore the design space systematically. An open-source, ML-accelerated tool fills a real and currently unmet need.

The decision-making the tool is designed to support is concrete: given a mission profile (e.g., "14-day equatorial mare traverse, 10 kg mass budget, 5 km traverse distance"), produce a Pareto front of feasible rover designs trading range against mass against slope capability, identify which design variables matter most via SHAP-based feature importance (Lundberg & Lee, 2017), and let the user interactively explore the tradespace via parametric sweeps. The tool is a *decision support* artifact, not a *decision making* artifact — its job is to give a designer or small team the same kind of rapid feedback that a Team X session provides, but without requiring access to JPL's facilities or its expert staff.

**Intended users.** Academic mission concept teams, graduate students conducting thesis-level rover design studies, and small commercial lunar mobility startups that lack access to proprietary concurrent-engineering facilities but need to perform credible early-phase trade studies.

## 3. Project Scope

The project aims to deliver three artifacts: (1) an open-source Python mission evaluator with every sub-model implemented from a cited published source, (2) a trained ML surrogate over the coupled design space with calibrated uncertainty quantification, and (3) a packaged tradespace exploration tool with NSGA-II multi-objective optimization, SHAP-based design-rule extraction, and a validation suite that demonstrates the tool can rediscover the published design points of real lunar micro-rovers (Rashid, Pragyan, and Yutu-class) when given matching mission constraints. The complete schedule, sub-model specifications, and validation strategy are documented in the companion `roverdevkit_project_plan.md`.

**Prototyping approach.** The mission evaluator is built first and stands alone as a useful tool independent of any ML work, which protects against ML-side risk. Sub-models are implemented in dependency order (terramechanics → power → mass → traverse simulator → thermal check) and validated independently against published data before being integrated. The ML surrogate is layered on top of the working evaluator only after Week 5, when end-to-end real-rover validation has confirmed the evaluator produces sensible predictions. This ordering ensures that if any phase fails, the prior phases still produce a publishable artifact.

**Data assumptions.** Each sub-model draws on a specific published data source for its model form, its parameter values, and its independent validation. The table below summarizes the anticipated sources; every entry will be cited explicitly in the final paper and tracked in the project repository.

| Sub-model | Model form (source) | Parameter data | Validation data |
|---|---|---|---|
| Terramechanics (analytical) | Bekker-Wong pressure-sinkage (Wong, 2008; Bekker, 1969) with Janosi-Hanamoto shear (Janosi & Hanamoto, 1961), from Wong's *Theory of Ground Vehicles* | Published bevameter tests on lunar simulants (FJS-1, JSC-1A, GRC-1); bulk regolith properties from the *Lunar Sourcebook* (Heiken et al., 1991) | Single-wheel testbed data from Ding et al. (2011); Wong's own datasets (Wong, 2008) |
| Terramechanics (SCM correction) | PyChrono SCM single-wheel simulations (Serban et al., 2023), wrapped in a learned correction model | ~2,000 SCM runs generated on the development laptop | Same single-wheel testbed data as the analytical layer (Ding et al., 2011); also cross-checked against Bekker-Wong trends |
| Solar power | Standard solar geometry and panel efficiency modeling from Larson & Wertz, *Space Mission Analysis and Design* (Wertz et al., 2011) | Lunar sun geometry from NASA ephemeris data; panel parameters and dust degradation factors from Yutu-2 and VIPER concept publications | Published noon-power and diurnal profiles for Yutu-2 |
| Battery | SOC differential equation with charge/discharge efficiency and temperature derating | Lithium-ion datasheet curves; NASA Glenn reports on Li-ion performance at lunar temperatures | Datasheet-level agreement check (e.g., 100 Wh → ~85 Wh usable) |
| Mass model | Parametric mass-estimating relationships (linear and power-law regression), following the MER methodology in *SMAD* (Wertz et al., 2011) | Published specs for ~8 lunar micro-rovers: Rashid, Pragyan, Yutu-1/2, CADRE, Sojourner, Lunokhod, plus 1–2 academic concept rovers | Leave-one-out cross-validation on the small dataset; sensitivity analysis on regression coefficients |
| Traverse simulator | Time-stepped numerical integrator (forward Euler / RK4) that calls the other sub-models in a loop | Numerical settings (timestep, tolerances) chosen from standard numerical practice; mission scenario inputs (latitude, traverse distance, terrain class, sun geometry) drawn from Apollo site descriptions, Yutu-2 traverse logs, and VIPER-class polar mission concept reports | Daily traverse distance and power profile for Yutu-2 compared to published mission data |
| Thermal check | Lumped-parameter heat balance from Gilmore, *Spacecraft Thermal Control Handbook* (Gilmore, 2002) | Absorptivity/emissivity values from standard spacecraft materials tables (Gilmore, 2002); RHU specs from NASA Glenn reports | Binary pass/fail sanity check against published Yutu-2 and Pragyan thermal survival data |
| Top-level ML surrogate | Gradient-boosted trees (XGBoost) (Chen & Guestrin, 2016) and small neural network ensemble | ~50,000 LHS samples from the analytical mission evaluator; ~2,000 multi-fidelity samples if SCM path is active | Held-out test set from the evaluator (10%); end-to-end comparison to published rover traverse data |

The overall data strategy is **physics and published experiments for the sub-models; large synthetic datasets from the evaluator for the top-level surrogate**. No sub-model depends on data that is proprietary, paywalled, or unpublished, which is a deliberate choice to protect reproducibility. The small-sample limitation of the mass model is the weakest link in the data story and is documented explicitly in Section 4.

**What the surrogate predicts.** The top-level surrogate is a multi-output regression model that maps a design vector and a mission scenario directly to eight mission-level output quantities: *range* (km traversed before the mission terminates), *energy margin* (minimum battery state-of-charge reached during the mission), *slope capability* (maximum climbable slope under worst-case soil conditions), *total mass* (kg), *peak motor torque* (N·m), *maximum sinkage* (m), and two binary constraint flags for *thermal survival* and *motor torque feasibility*. These are end-of-mission scalar summaries, not time-series trajectories — a deliberate simplification chosen because the downstream consumer (NSGA-II) operates on scalar objectives, and because scalar regression is far more sample-efficient than trajectory prediction. Users who need the full state trajectory (e.g., the battery SOC curve over a 14-day traverse) can always fall back to the mission evaluator itself; the surrogate exists specifically to accelerate the cases where scalar outcomes are sufficient.

**Tasks** are organized into four phases over 15 weeks: Phase 1 (weeks 1–5) builds and validates the mission evaluator; Phase 2 (weeks 6–9) generates training data and trains the surrogate; Phase 3 (weeks 10–12) builds the tradespace exploration tool and runs the rediscovery validation; Phase 4 (weeks 13–15) drafts the paper.

**Deliverables.**

- An open-source Python package (`roverdevkit/`) containing the mission evaluator, all sub-models, the trained surrogate, and the tradespace exploration tools, released under an MIT license with installation instructions and API documentation.
- A pre-trained surrogate model shipped with the package so users can perform tradespace exploration without installing PyChrono or regenerating training data.
- A validation suite documenting (a) sub-model accuracy against published experimental data, (b) end-to-end mission evaluator accuracy against real rover traverse data for Yutu-2, Pragyan, and Rashid, and (c) the rediscovery validation showing the optimizer recovers real rover design points within stated tolerances.
- Three Jupyter notebooks: an interactive tradespace exploration tutorial, a Pareto-front-generation walkthrough for the four mission scenarios, and a paper-figure reproduction notebook.
- A submission-ready manuscript targeting *Journal of Field Robotics*, *Journal of Spacecraft and Rockets*, or *Acta Astronautica*, with IEEE Aerospace or AIAA SciTech as fallback venues.

## 4. Exclusions and Limitations

Several methods and modeling choices are deliberately excluded from the first version of the tool, in each case because including them would either inflate the scope past what is achievable in 15 weeks on a laptop or would weaken the verifiability of the result.

**Discrete Element Method (DEM) terramechanics is excluded.** DEM with resolved granular particles produces the highest-fidelity wheel-soil interaction data but at compute costs of hours per simulated second on a 20-core workstation (Serban et al., 2023), which is incompatible with running on a MacBook. The Continuum Representation Model (CRM) in Chrono is also excluded because it requires an NVIDIA GPU, which the development machine does not have. The tool relies on the Soil Contact Model (SCM) — a semi-empirical generalization of Bekker-Wong — as its highest-fidelity terramechanics layer.

**Thermal architecture is not a design variable in v1.** It is checked as a binary survival constraint (does the avionics box stay within survivable temperature limits during peak sun and lunar night?) using a lumped-parameter model, not optimized as a continuous design variable. Adding thermal as a full design dimension is a stretch goal contingent on the rest of the project staying on schedule.

**The tool targets micro-rovers (5–50 kg), not VIPER-class or human-rated rovers.** This is a deliberate scoping choice driven by the validation strategy: there are several recent flown lunar micro-rovers with publicly available specifications (Rashid, Pragyan, Yutu-1/2, CADRE), which is the minimum population needed to fit parametric mass models and to perform the rediscovery validation. Larger rovers have proprietary mass models and are not validatable in the same way.

**The mass model has small-sample limitations.** Fitting parametric mass-estimating relationships from ~8 published rovers produces loose fits that capture trends but not absolute values. This is documented as a known limitation, the rediscovery validation tolerances are set accordingly (within ~25–30% on key dimensions), and sensitivity to mass model uncertainty is reported as part of the robustness analysis.

**Reduced-gravity scaling effects are approximated, not modeled from first principles.** The tool uses lunar gravity (1.62 m/s²) as a constant in the terramechanics calculations and trusts that Bekker-Wong soil parameters calibrated against terrestrial bevameter tests on lunar simulants approximate lunar conditions adequately. This is the standard approximation in published rover design work but is not a substitute for in-situ data.

**The tool does not address autonomy, navigation, fault management, or science instrument design.** It is strictly a mobility-power co-design tool. Integrating autonomy considerations (e.g., the cost of obstacle avoidance on traverse efficiency) is a natural extension but is out of scope.

Finally, the project is **explicitly not an ML methods contribution.** The novelty is in the integration of a verifiable physics-based mission evaluator with a surrogate-driven tradespace exploration workflow and the rediscovery validation against real flown rovers. The ML methods used (gradient-boosted trees, NSGA-II, SHAP) are standard. Reviewers expecting a novel ML algorithm will be disappointed; reviewers looking for an open-source, verifiable tool that fills a real gap in academic rover design should find the contribution clear.
